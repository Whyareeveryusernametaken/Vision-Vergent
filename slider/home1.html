Neurodiversity

In a non-pathological meaning, neurodiversity, or ND, refers to variance in the human brain regarding sociability, learning, attention, mood, and other mental activities. It was coined in 1998 by sociologist Judy Singer, who, together with writer Harvey Blume, helped popularise the term. It arose as a response to popular belief that many conditions now categorized as neurodevelopmental disorders are essentially pathological, and instead embrace the social model of disability, in which societal constraints are the primary cause of people's handicaps.

Within the autism rights movement, this viewpoint is very common. The resulting neurodiversity paradigm has sparked debate among disability advocates, with critics claiming that it is based on wishful thinking and may minimize, ignore, or even romanticize serious mental illnesses, as well as claiming that it is largely endorsed by those with low support needs and does not reflect the realities of those with higher support needs.

Causes
Causes of neurodevelopmental conditions include:
  • Genetics
  • Trauma at birth
  • Infectious disease
  • Immune disorders
  • Nutritional factors
  • Physical trauma
